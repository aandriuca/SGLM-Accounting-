The Two-Speed Portfolio Problem: Ontario's Rent Gap

Toronto one-bedroom asking rents dropped $162 year-over-year. The provincial rent increase guideline sits at 2.5%. That gap between what you can charge a new tenant and what you can charge an existing one is widening — and most property owners aren't measuring it unit by unit.

When every unit looks fine in aggregate but NOI quietly shrinks, the problem is almost never the portfolio. It's the visibility.

The owners making deliberate decisions right now — where to hold, where to offer incentives, where to revisit underwriting — can see their vacant-unit turn rate, their incentive spend per new lease, and their occupied-unit yield side by side. Without that view, the gap doesn't stay still. It compounds.

Your numbers should show you exactly where you stand — before the gap does the deciding for you.
